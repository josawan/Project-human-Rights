History of Human Rights

The history of human rights is a long journey of struggle for dignity, freedom, and equality. Since ancient times, people have sought to establish rules that protect individuals from injustice and oppression, but the modern concept of human rights emerged only after centuries of social, political, and philosophical development. The idea began in ancient civilizations such as Babylon, Egypt, and Greece, then evolved during the Middle Ages with the Magna Carta in England, and reached new heights after the European revolutions and the American Declaration of Independence. With the adoption of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in 1948, human rights became a global principle recognized by all nations. Studying the history of human rights helps us appreciate their true value and understand the long struggle that made these rights possible.

  • The Magna Carta

    Although the Magna Carta (The Great Charter), issued in England in 1215, was essentially an agreement aimed at resolving a conflict between King John and the feudal barons, it transcended its narrow context to become a cornerstone in the construction of the idea of human rights. It was not about "human rights" in the contemporary sense, but it planted the seeds of principles that became fundamental to it. It did not achieve equality for all, but it established the principle that power must be constrained by law, Clause 39 states: "No free man shall be seized or imprisoned, or stripped of his rights or possessions except by the lawful judgement of his peers or by the law of the land Connection to Human Rights: This is the foundation upon which all modern charters are built. It limits the arbitrariness of power and ensures that everyone – from the ruler to the ruled – is subject to the law. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (Article 7) states that "All are equal before the law, The aforementioned Clause 39 contained the seed of the concept of due process of law ,the right to be tried by one's lawful judge and not by a special tribunal, and not to be imprisoned or punished without a trial.Connection to Human Rights: This right is the cornerstone of modern criminal justice. It is elaborated in Article 10 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights Everyone is entitled in full equality to a fair and public hearing by an independent and impartial tribunal, The document itself was a commitment by the king that his power was not absolute. It was a contract between the ruler and the ruled, although the ruled at that time were only the class of nobles, The idea that the state must commit to respecting the rights and freedoms of its citizens and must not abuse its power is the central idea in international human rights law, Rights are boundaries that the state must not cross. It is important to note that the Magna Carta was not a universal declaration of human rights in the modern sense, for the following reasons, Narrow Scope: The term free man in its texts excluded the vast majority of the population, such as peasants (serfs) and women ,Class Motives: It was created to solve a political crisis between the ruling elite and the king, not out of a belief in human equality, Despite its historical limitations, the Magna Carta remains a pivotal moment in human history. It transformed the concept of rights from a grant given by the ruler into a legal claim that could be defended against his arbitrariness The seeds it planted the rule of law, the right to a fair trial, and the limits of power grew and branched out over the centuries to eventually bear fruit in universal documents like the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948). Therefore, we can say that the Magna Carta was the historical and ethical nucleus from which the concept of human rights, as we know it today, emerged.

  • The Cyrus Cylinder

    In a time when power was the only language among kings Cyrus the Great the king of Persia came with a completely different vision He did not rely on war or force but believed in justice and freedom and expressed these values in a historic document known as the Cyrus Cylinder The Cyrus Cylinder is a clay artifact written in 539 BC after Cyrus conquered Babylon , It contains his decisions which were revolutionary for that time, Freedom for nations and return of captives to their homelands, Freedom of religion and belief for everyone, Equality between races and peoples, Because of these principles the cylinder is considered the first declaration of human rights more than 2500 years before modern laws, Today it is displayed in the British Museum as a global symbol of freedom and human dignity, The Cyrus Cylinder shows that justice is stronger than power and that a true ruler leads with mercy not fear, It remains a message from the past reminding us that respect for people is the foundation of every civilization

  • The UN Charter

    The UN Charter, signed in 1945, is the main document that established the United Nations after World War II. Its purpose was to maintain international peace and security, but it also placed great focus on human rights and the dignity of every person. The Charter recognized that peace cannot exist without equality, freedom, and justice, In the Preamble, it mentions faith in fundamental human rights, in the dignity and worth of the human person, and in the equal rights of men and women and of nations large and small, This shows that promoting human rights was a key goal of the UN from the start, Several articles clearly highlight this idea

  • Article 1 (3): Promotes respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms for all people, without discrimination.
  • Articles 55 and 56: Call on member states to cooperate to improve living standards and ensure universal respect for human rights.
  • Articles 62 and 68: Give the Economic and Social Council the authority to recommend actions and create commissions to promote human rights worldwide. These principles became the foundation of international human rights law and inspired the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) in 1948, along with the creation of organizations like the UN Human Rights Council, the UN Charter made human rights a central part of achieving global peace, equality, and respect for every human being.

    conclusion

    In conclusion, the history of human rights shows that these rights were not granted as a gift but earned through centuries of human struggle against injustice and oppression. Each era of history contributed a new step toward defining and protecting human dignity. Today, it is our responsibility to preserve what has been achieved and continue defending these rights against any threat, for the respect of human rights remains the foundation of peace and justice in the world.
